AI usability testing
How we used AI to test and review CleanPath — and what it surfaced.
AI usability review · User research

We asked AI to become our user

Then we handed her our live app and asked her to think aloud.

Karin Lindqvist, 41

Karin Lindqvist, 41, is an Environmental Health Officer in a Stockholm district. She owns water quality for ~8 bathing sites and is first responder for air, heat, fire, and rainfall. Experienced but time-pressed, she's accountable for every advisory she issues — so she needs data she can trust and a record she can defend.

We asked Claude to construct a credible user persona — Karin — and then roleplay as her doing a moderated think-aloud on the live deployed app. The goal: surface real usability issues without needing to recruit a real user for this sprint.
AI usability review · Karin's feedback
Karin Lindqvist · Environmental Health Officer

The workflow is exactly how I think — observe, assess, decide, communicate, log. But before I send something the public sees, I need to be very sure — and right now nothing makes me stop and check.

— Karin, moderated think-aloud session
3 Serious
4 Moderate
3 Minor
This was Karin's headline feedback. She validated the core workflow logic — but flagged that the most consequential action (sending a public advisory) had no confirmation gate. 3 serious, 4 moderate, 3 minor issues surfaced in total.
AI design critique · Design director

Then we hired an AI design director

AI Design Director

You've built something with real taste and a coherent token system — but pointed it in a direction that quietly works against the product's job.

It looks like a calm wellness app. But it is a decision instrument for an officer acting on public-safety hazards and it needs to reflect that.

North star
"Calm until it isn't." Hold the warmth for the resting state — then build a deliberate escalation so the interface visibly tightens as hazard and uncertainty rise.
We gave Claude a design-director persona and asked it to critique CleanPath as a senior creative would — not just list issues but name the underlying tension. The thesis: the product has taste, but its visual language is calibrated for calm when it should escalate.
