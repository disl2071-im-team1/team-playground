Where the numbers come from

Open data
Bullerkartan 2022
Stockholm Stad · via ArcGIS REST API

Stockholm's official noise map, published by the city and updated annually. It models sound levels from road traffic, rail, and air traffic across every street segment. Quiet Route uses this to score each route and flag zones above 80 dB.

Noise sources · equally weighted
Road traffic
Cars, buses, and trucks. The dominant source in central Stockholm, peaking during rush hours.
Rail traffic
T-bana, commuter trains, and trams. Concentrated near stations and along rail corridors.
Air traffic
Flight paths over the city, mainly affecting northern Stockholm near Arlanda approach routes.
Decibel reference scale
Below 55 dB — Quiet streets, parks, residential paths
55–65 dB — Normal conversation level, moderate roads
65–75 dB — Busy streets, near tram stops
75–80 dB — Heavy traffic corridors
Above 80 dB — Threshold. Reroute alert fires here.
The 80 dB threshold is the level at which prolonged exposure begins to carry risk. WHO guidelines recommend keeping environmental noise below 53 dB (daytime) for residential areas.
% noise reduction · how it's calculated
Reference dB = average of Fastest and Cleanest route dB
Reduction = (Reference dB − Quietest dB) ÷ Reference dB × 100
Example: (76 + 65) ÷ 2 = 70.5 → (70.5 − 55) ÷ 70.5 = 22% quieter

The comparison is always dynamic. If you change transport mode or the underlying data updates, the percentage recalculates automatically.
