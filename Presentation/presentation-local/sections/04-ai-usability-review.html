AI usability review

We had AI run the usability test, as a persona

Most of our energy went into the AI workflow, not the product itself. So we tested the product the same way — an AI moderated a think-aloud session in character, against the live deployed build.

The persona
"Karin" — Environmental Health Officer
Owns ~8 bathing sites in a Stockholm district. Not a dashboard power-user — and personally accountable for every public advisory she issues.
The method
Moderated think-aloud + live walkthrough
Values, provenance, and the advisory workflow were read straight from the deployed build; the "send" gate was verified by instrumentation.
Headline insight
Stale, low-confidence data → a confidently worded "safe to swim" notice → one ungated click → published to the public. That chain is the priority to break.

S1

The most consequential, irreversible action in the tool is completely ungated — one click goes straight to "sent."
Fix: Medium

Fix: Medium

Strengths to protect
One consistent map-driven workflow across all five hazards · best-in-class provenance and confidence honesty · an excellent, attributed audit trail.
Three serious findings cluster at the highest-stakes moment — publishing a public advisory. (1) No confirm or preview before a public advisory goes out. (2) The tool's internal "low confidence / 17 days overdue" honesty is stripped out of the generated public message. (3) A one-click "clear shift history" sits right next to Export with no undo, next to the audit trail that is the officer's whole defensibility. Close on the strengths worth protecting: one consistent map-driven workflow, best-in-class provenance, and an excellent audit trail.
Design critique · From an AI design director

Real taste, pointed against the product's job

It looks like a calm consumer wellness app — but it's a decision instrument for an officer accountable for public-safety calls. At the moments that matter most, the visuals whisper when they should speak.

The gap to close
A 17-day-stale "Advisory" badge has the visual temperature of a spa booking confirmation.
North star
"Calm until it isn't." Hold the warmth for the resting state — then build a deliberate escalation so the interface visibly tightens as hazard and uncertainty rise.
Pivot from usability to a design-director critique. The thesis: we have real taste and a coherent token system, but pointed it in a direction that works against the product's job. Clean Path looks like a calm consumer wellness app, but it's a decision instrument for an accountable officer. The line that lands: a 17-day-stale advisory badge currently has the visual temperature of a spa booking confirmation. North star for the next iteration: "calm until it isn't." Keep the warmth and the strengths — the work is making the visuals mean the stakes.
The direction for next iteration

Make the visuals mean the stakes

01
Unify the risk-color language. One escalating system across map, pills and badges — and let severity actually feel urgent.
02
Match affordance to consequence. Make destructive and irreversible-public actions visually distinct and gated; quiet the routine ones.
03
Rebuild the type ladder. Lift body text to ~14–15px and retire the 10px operational text — earn hierarchy through weight, not a size cliff.
04
Promote triage. A persistent cross-hazard at-a-glance strip — with data age made visual, so an old reading looks old.
05
Elevate provenance, warm the chrome. Make confidence a signature, not a footnote — and rename "Decision surface" to language Karin would use.
Five priorities for the next iteration, in the director's order. (1) Unify risk color into one escalating system across map, pills and badges — let severity feel urgent. (2) Re-weight action affordances to match consequence: distinguish and gate destructive and irreversible-public actions, quiet the rest. (3) Rebuild the type ladder, lift body text to ~14-15px, kill the 10px operational text. (4) Promote triage — a persistent cross-hazard at-a-glance strip with data age made visual. (5) Elevate provenance from footnote to signature, and warm up the chrome naming to match the brand.
